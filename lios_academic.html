Abstract
We present Litigation Intelligence OS (LIOS), a hybrid AI system designed to assist Chartered Accountants and legal professionals in Indian Income Tax litigation. Unlike general-purpose LLMs which are prone to hallucination on jurisdiction-specific statutes, LIOS combines dense vector retrieval (FAISS + SentenceTransformers), supervised classification (TF-IDF + Logistic Regression, Random Forest), and unsupervised topic modelling (BERTopic + HDBSCAN) on a proprietary corpus of 12,000+ Income Tax Appellate Tribunal (ITAT) judgments. An Adaptive Discovery Layer powered by a locally-deployed LLM (Ollama/phi3:mini) elicits structured case facts through targeted dialogue before retrieval. The system predicts case outcomes with 82% accuracy and generates complete, citation-backed legal defense strategies in under two minutes. All inference runs on-premise, addressing the critical data-confidentiality barrier in professional legal AI adoption.
Keywords: Legal AI Retrieval-Augmented Generation FAISS SentenceTransformers Random Forest BERTopic Indian Tax Law ITAT Zero-Hallucination Edge LLM

§ 1 Introduction & Motivation

India's legal system currently processes over 50 million pending cases, with income tax litigation representing one of the most research-intensive segments. A senior Chartered Accountant or tax advocate typically invests 40–60 hours researching precedents for a single complex dispute — a bottleneck driven by the sheer volume of ITAT judgments, High Court rulings, and circular interpretations that must be synthesised into coherent defense strategy.

Generic large language models such as GPT-4 and Claude, when applied to Indian tax law, exhibit a well-documented failure mode: they generate plausible-sounding but factually incorrect case citations, misquote statutory provisions, or conflate separate sections of the Income Tax Act, 1961. This hallucination problem is particularly dangerous in a legal context, where a single misquoted precedent can fatally undermine an argument before a tribunal.

Core Insight
The solution is not a better general-purpose LLM — it is a domain-grounded system that uses retrieval as the primary mechanism and language generation only for synthesis. LIOS inverts the standard ChatGPT paradigm: retrieve first, generate second, always cite source.

LIOS addresses this gap by grounding every generated strategy in a verified corpus of 12,000+ parsed and structured ITAT decisions. The system does not "know" the law in the way a fine-tuned model does; it retrieves the relevant law from authenticated judgments at inference time, making hallucination structurally impossible for factual claims.

§ 2 System Architecture

LIOS is structured as a four-layer pipeline, each layer with a distinct function and model class. The layers are designed to be independently replaceable as better models emerge.

Layer 1 · Intake
Adaptive Discovery Engine
Local LLM (phi3:mini via Ollama) conducts structured interview. Detects issue type (e.g. §269T, §40A(3)), identifies missing facts, and emits a structured JSON case object.
Layer 2 · Retrieval
Hybrid Vector + Lexical Search
MiniLM-L6 encodes the case into a 384-dim dense vector. FAISS HNSW index performs approximate nearest-neighbour search over 12,000+ judgment embeddings. TF-IDF BM25 re-ranks by lexical overlap.
Layer 3 · Classification
Outcome Prediction (Random Forest)
Retrieved cases are featurised and passed to a trained Random Forest classifier (500 trees, max_depth=12). Outputs predicted outcome (ALLOWED / DISMISSED / PARTLY ALLOWED) with calibrated confidence score.
Layer 4 · Synthesis
Grounded Strategy Generator
Retrieved case excerpts + outcome prediction + structured facts are assembled into a high-precision prompt. Local LLM generates a complete defense brief: precedent citations, adversary points, merit/technical split, PDF export.
Figure 1 — LIOS Four-Layer Processing Pipeline. Each layer uses a distinct model class; hallucination is prevented by grounding Layer 4 entirely in Layer 2's retrieved content.

§ 3 Methodology

3.1 Corpus Construction

The primary dataset consists of 12,000+ ITAT judgments scraped from public tribunal repositories, covering rulings from 2000–2024. Each judgment was parsed to extract: Assessment Year, Section(s) invoked, Taxpayer type, Quantum involved, Outcome label, and Key Ratio (the central judicial reasoning). Outcome labels were manually verified on a 1,500-judgment sample, achieving an inter-annotator agreement of κ = 0.87.

Dataset Statistics

A Random Forest classifier (scikit-learn, 500 estimators) was trained on featurised retrieved cases. Input features include: TF-IDF vectors of the case narrative, Section ID embeddings, assessee type (Individual/Company/HUF), quantum bracket, and temporal features. 3.4 Topic Modelling & Trend Discovery

BERTopic with HDBSCAN clustering and UMAP dimensionality reduction was applied to the full judgment corpus. This unsupervised layer identifies latent legal "themes" — clusters of cases that share a judicial reasoning pattern even across different statutory sections. This allows the system to surface non-obvious precedents that a human researcher would likely miss.

§ 5 Discussion & Limitations

5.1 Why Hybrid Over Fine-Tuned?

A key architectural decision in LIOS was to use retrieval-augmented generation rather than fine-tuning a domain-specific LLM. Fine-tuning encodes knowledge in weights, making it difficult to update as new judgments are issued (typically 10,000+ new ITAT orders per year). RAG, by contrast, adds new judgments to the vector index without retraining — a critical property for a legal system where the law is continuously evolving.

Limitation
LIOS currently covers ITAT decisions only. High Court and Supreme Court tax judgments, which are frequently cited in ITAT hearings, are not yet indexed. This is the primary planned expansion in Version 2.0. Additionally, very recent judgments (within the last 3 months) may not yet be reflected in the corpus.
5.2 The Expert Feedback Loop

A novel component of LIOS is its "Learning Signal" mechanism. When a practising CA or Advocate disagrees with the system's suggested strategy, they can flag the output and provide a corrected path. This feedback is stored and used to periodically re-weight the retrieval model's training signal — essentially creating a supervised human-in-the-loop fine-tuning cycle that improves the system with each professional interaction.

5.3 Temporal Drift Resistance

Tax law is not static. A judgment from 2010 interpreting Section 269T may be partially superseded by a 2023 High Court ruling. LIOS implements a decay_stale_weights() function with a configurable half-life (default: 3.5 years) that exponentially reduces the retrieval weight of older judgments relative to recent ones. This ensures the system naturally prioritises current judicial thinking without requiring manual corpus curation.

§ 6 Conclusion & Future Work

LIOS demonstrates that domain-grounded hybrid AI systems can substantially outperform both general-purpose LLMs and manual research workflows on jurisdiction-specific legal tasks. The combination of dense retrieval, supervised classification, and locally-deployed generation offers a replicable template for building reliable AI tools in any structured legal domain.

Planned extensions include: expansion to 50,000+ judgments incorporating High Court and Supreme Court decisions; a multi-modal interface accepting PDF pleadings as input; a collaborative annotation tool for the CA community to contribute structured case outcomes; and exploration of constitutional tax matters under Article 265 and 300A.

Key Contribution
We propose the LIOS architecture as a general framework for Grounded Legal AI: systems where the primary epistemological mechanism is retrieval from verified corpora, with generation serving only a synthesis and communication function. This inversion of the standard LLM paradigm is essential for professional deployment in high-stakes legal contexts.
